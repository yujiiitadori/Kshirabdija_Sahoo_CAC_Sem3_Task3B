Road Side School- Delhi

Shyam Bihari Prasad

A selfless retiree has set up a school on the roadside for some of New Delhi's poorest children. A passerby might mistake Shyam Bihari Prasad’s students for a ragged bunch of street beggars but this unremarkable pavement is an invaluable resource for children to educate themselves. The 67-year-old retired from his job in telecommunications in 2010 to look after his granddaughter with his wife.Three years ago he decided to set up the unconventional school to tackle the gaps in education for New Delhi's poorest class of children. He teaches his students Hindi, English, Science, Math and Social Sciences The retiree started with no mats to sit on, with only five students, teaching only orally. Over time, more students came and people living in the vicinity or just passing by donated mats books, pencils, boards and more which made up his school. During days of bad weather, a nearby temple allows him to teach the students inside.

Dangerous Ways to School

Twice a year, Father Latak undertakes the challenging journey to the boarding school with his ten-year-old son, Motup, crossing the notorious Chadar, the path over the cloak of ice. Before embarking, Father Latak carefully observes the sky, ensuring no storms loom. The family, along with three other students and Jordan's father Thinly, begins the trek, a perilous route fraught with risks. Motup, privileged to attend a better school in town, plays near their hut one last time before the journey. The family prepares with wool socks, prayers, and mantras for protection. The expedition requires complete trust in the fathers' experience. Walking cautiously on the frozen river, they face the challenges of early spring, risking thinning ice. Father Latak leads, testing the ice with a stick before each step. Navigating treacherous rocky paths, they encounter dangers from avalanches and melting ice. Finding shelter in a cave, they rest before tackling the most hazardous part. Negotiating a narrow strip of ice, Father Latak pulls Motup alongside. After four days of unpredictable obstacles, they reach Leh and the school. Fathers swiftly return, wary of the melting river's uncertainties on their way back.

Marginalized communities ofteleftce challenges such as lack of infrastructure, insufficient resources, and discrimination, hindering their access to quality education.

Education equips individuals with skills and knowledge, empowering them to secure better employment opportunities and escape the cycle of poverty.

Various NGOs, government programs, and international initiatives focus on providing scholarships, building schools, and implementing educational reforms to uplift marginalized communities.

Technology can facilitate e-learning, providing access to educational resources, online courses, and interactive tools, bridging the gap for marginalized communities in remote areas.

Cultural sensitivity ensures that educational programs respect and integrate the cultural context of marginalized communities, making the learning experience more relevant and effective.

Individuals can contribute by volunteering, supporting NGOs, donating educational resources, and advocating for policies that promote equal access to education.

Governments can invest in education infrastructure, allocate resources equitably, and implement policies that address systemic inequalities to ensure marginalized communities receive quality education.

Mentorship programs provide guidance, support, and role models, helping students from marginalized communities navigate educational challenges and make informed decisions about their future.

Customized curricula, language-sensitive teaching methods, and community involvement can ensure that education is adapted to the unique needs and circumstances of marginalized groups.

Indicators such as literacy rates, school enrollment, and skill development can be used to assess the effectiveness of educational initiatives and track the progress of marginalized communities over time.
